Neuroplasticity and Growth Mindset

Neuroplasticity

Neuroplasticity is the brain's ability to create neural pathways as we learn or adapt to new circumstances. Until recently, scientists believed that our brains stop changing as we grow older, and while neuroplasticity in children is more prominent, adults can still benefit from it. As we work on a skill or think about a new idea, our brains form new neural pathways that become stronger and stronger with repetition and eventually replace the old ones. Understanding that intelligence and ability are malleable can lead to higher academic achievements and even help when recovering from a brain injury.

Some of the more obvious ways to increase neuroplasticity are repetition, routine and practice. Moreover, having a clear idea on how our newfound knowledge is applicable not just in theory but also in real life is quite beneficial. Readjusting our approach with neuroplasticity in mind when encountering difficult tasks can drastically improve the results.

I would love to employ the principles of neuroplasticity in my own life, especially when attending Dev Academy's bootcamp. I am practicing coding every day and it is quite comforting to think that my brain is working very hard to readjust to all this new knowledge. I was very surprised when I learned that "virtual" training or just thinking about an activity uses the same parts of the brain responsible for actually performing it.

Growth Mindset

The idea of growth vs fixed mindset has been developed and popularised by Carol Dweck, a psychology professor at Stanford University. The concept of a "fixed mindset" revolves around static intelligence and innate talent or ability. People with fixed mindset tend to avoid challenge, seek praise for their current achievements and fear making mistakes or being seen as unintelligent. On the other hand, people with "growth mindset" believe that with effort everyone can improve their intelligence and skills, while embracing challenges as a chance to learn more.

While the theory draws some critique, especially when applied in academia, it could be very beneficial on an individual level. I am consciously trying to shift my mindset away from some of my fixed traits. I do tend to blame myself for being a failure when engaging with something beyond my current ability and that often makes me quit, but I am embracing the "it's an opportunity for growth" point of view.

Learning plan and strategies

While it is quite difficult to change your ways as an adult, I hope that understanding the science behind our brains can help me form new habits/ways of thinking and stay persistent. Neuroscience has always been a bit of a hobby for me, so reading up on topics like these helps me to get through the harder bits of learning.
